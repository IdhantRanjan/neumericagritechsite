## The other grain price

Two numbers decide what your corn is worth: the futures price everyone
watches, and the one that actually hits your check — the cash bid at your
elevator. The gap between them is **basis**, and it moves for its own
reasons.

Futures reflect the national and global balance sheet. Basis reflects
*your* market: how full local elevators are, what rail and barge freight
costs, how hungry the nearest processor or ethanol plant is this month,
and how much grain your neighbors are moving.

That independence is the point. A week where futures rally 20¢ while your
local basis widens 25¢ is a week your price *fell* even though the screen
looked great. Selling on a futures rally without checking basis is how that
happens — and it's invisible unless you track both.

Three numbers worth writing down for your own market:

1. **Your typical harvest basis** — the seasonal wide point, when everyone
   delivers at once.
2. **Your typical spring/summer basis** — usually tighter, when elevators
   are emptying out. The difference between 1 and 2 is what storage is
   really competing against, alongside interest and shrink.
3. **Today's basis** — cash bid minus the nearby futures. Thirty seconds on
   the elevator's website.

If today's number is unusually tight against your history, the local
market is asking for grain now. Unusually wide — it's telling you it has
plenty. Neither is a prediction of anything; both are information most
selling decisions ignore.
