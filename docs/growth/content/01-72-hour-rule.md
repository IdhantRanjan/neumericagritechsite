## The 72-hour rule: the deadline that eats claims

Most crop-insurance deadlines are printed on calendars. The one that
denies otherwise-valid claims isn't a date at all — it's a stopwatch.

Federal crop insurance requires you to give **notice of loss within 72
hours of discovering damage** (and in writing within 15 days). Hail on
Saturday night, discovered Sunday morning — your clock is already running,
weekend or not.

What makes this rule dangerous is that it arrives on the worst day of your
season. There's water in the shop, the generator's out, family's calling —
and the *paperwork* clock is the thing you're least likely to think about.

Three habits that protect the claim:

**1. The call counts.** Notice can start with a phone call to your agent.
Make it before cleanup, write down when you called and who you talked to.

**2. Photograph before you touch anything.** Wide shots that show the
extent, close-ups that show the mechanism (hail bruising, lodged stalks,
water lines). Your phone timestamps and geotags them — that metadata is
evidence.

**3. Don't destroy the evidence.** Adjusters need representative strips of
the damage. Tilling under a ruined field before appraisal can void the
claim entirely. If you must act (safety, drainage), get your adjuster's
sign-off in writing first.

The pattern across denied claims isn't fraud or bad farming — it's honest
people cleaning up fast and documenting late. The paperwork standard is
unfair in exactly that way, which is why the discipline has to be built
before the storm.
